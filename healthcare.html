For Healthcare

When AI touches a care surface, humans need standing in the record.

When AI touches a care surface, the record should show what changed, why it was allowed, and who had standing to say yes. Healthcare AI can update records, route tasks, prepare messages, and move information across systems. Those actions need boundaries before they become institutional fact.

Scope first

This is about action surfaces, not clinical judgment.

ZLAR does not diagnose, treat, recommend care, or validate clinical decisions. The healthcare conversation here is narrower and more concrete: when an AI-enabled workflow tries to touch a record, route a task, send a message, access a file, or move data, where is the boundary?

The care record is consequence. The question is who had authority at the moment the action crossed into it.

The affected person may not be in the approval loop. That is why the record matters.

What the receipt gives

Operators and affected people need inspectable records.

Attempt

What changed or moved?

A governed receipt starts with the action the workflow attempted, not a vague AI explanation.

Authority

Who had standing?

Policy may allow, deny, or ask a named human before the governed action proceeds.

Verification

Is the record intact?

VALID means the record still verifies. It does not mean the action was clinically or legally correct.

Safe pilot posture

Start with a bounded administrative or records workflow.

The right first healthcare conversation is not a broad claim about clinical AI. It is a bounded workflow where the action surface is known, the policy owner is named, and the institution can inspect exactly what ZLAR sees and what it does not.
