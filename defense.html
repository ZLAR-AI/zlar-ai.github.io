For Defense / Military

Military AI needs delegation without surrender.

Military AI needs delegation without surrender. Autonomy is useful only when authority remains legible. If an agent can act across systems, the operating question is not whether it is intelligent. The question is where command, policy, and evidence stand when it tries to create consequence.

The risk

The danger is silent authority widening.

An agent starts with a task and accumulates capability through tools, integrations, context, and workflow permissions. Serious operators need to know where action is stopped, allowed, escalated, and recorded.

ZLAR is not another model judging a model. It is a deterministic boundary for routed/intercepted actions.

Autonomy changes command only if consequence can move without standing.

The boundary

Routed actions cross policy before consequence.

Policy

Default-deny posture

Signed policy decides whether a governed action can proceed, must stop, or must ask a human.

Authority

Named human standing

Consequential actions can require explicit authorization outside the agent runtime.

Evidence

Reviewable receipts

A receipt preserves what crossed the boundary, what policy applied, and what authority decided.

Deployment reality

This is an execution-boundary primitive, not a complete defense system.

Defense and military environments require bypass closure, key custody, network controls, platform controls, audit handling, and operational doctrine. ZLAR gives the routed action boundary a working form; the deployment decides how that boundary is made authoritative.

Boundary

  • ZLAR governs routed/intercepted action surfaces only.
  • ZLAR does not claim military approval, classified deployment status, acquisition readiness, safety assurance, or contested-environment coverage.
  • Receipts prove a governance event was recorded. They do not prove the action was correct or operationally appropriate.
  • External non-Vincent verifier attestation remains prepared/pending unless state changes.
